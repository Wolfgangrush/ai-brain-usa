## Currency Warning
The CFPB (Consumer Financial Protection Bureau) frequently updates "TRID" (TILA-RESPA Integrated Disclosure) rules. Ensure checking the latest "Small Entity Compliance Guide" on the CFPB website for current disclosure requirements.

## Key Facts
1. **Statute of Frauds:** Every state requires that a contract for the sale of real estate be in writing and signed by the party against whom enforcement is sought.
2. **RESPA (Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act):** 12 U.S.C. § 2601. Prohibits "kickbacks" for settlement service referrals and requires specific disclosures (Loan Estimate and Closing Disclosure).
3. **TILA (Truth in Lending Act):** 15 U.S.C. § 1601. Requires uniform disclosure of credit terms (APR) to allow consumers to compare loans.
4. **Recording Acts:**
   - **Race:** The first to record the deed wins.
   - **Notice:** A subsequent purchaser wins if they had no notice of the prior unrecorded interest.
   - **Race-Notice:** A subsequent purchaser wins only if they had no notice AND recorded first.
5. **Title Insurance:** Standard practice in the U.S. to protect against "defects" in title (liens, encumbrances, or competing claims).
6. **Closing (Settlement):** The final step where the deed is delivered, the purchase price is paid, and the title is recorded.
7. **Fair Housing Act:** 42 U.S.C. § 3601. Prohibits discrimination in the sale, rental, or financing of dwellings based on race, color, religion, sex, familial status, or national origin.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
Solo advocates acting as "Closing Attorneys" or "Title Agents" must be extremely diligent with "Escrow" and "Trust" accounts. Misappropriation of client funds during a real estate closing is a leading cause of disbarment. Mastery of the CFPB's disclosure timelines is also essential to avoid "delayed closings" which can result in breach of contract claims.
