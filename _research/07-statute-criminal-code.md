## Currency Warning
Federal criminal law is subject to frequent amendments by Congress. Notable recent changes include the **First Step Act** (sentencing reform) and various updates to cybercrime statutes.

## Key Facts
1. **Scope:** Title 18 contains most federal crimes, including wire fraud, mail fraud, racketeering (RICO), and federal drug offenses.
2. **Mens Rea:** Most federal crimes require a specific "intent" (knowingly, willfully), though some regulatory offenses are strict liability.
3. **Commonly Charged Offenses:**
   - **Wire Fraud (18 U.S.C. § 1343):** Broadly applied to any scheme to defraud involving interstate wires.
   - **Conspiracy (18 U.S.C. § 371):** Agreement between two or more persons to commit a federal crime.
   - **RICO (18 U.S.C. § 1961):** Targeting "enterprises" involved in a pattern of racketeering activity.
4. **Sentencing Guidelines:** While advisory (after *United States v. Booker*), the U.S. Sentencing Guidelines (USSG) are the primary tool used by judges to determine federal sentences.
5. **Jurisdiction:** Federal crimes must have a "federal nexus" (e.g., interstate commerce, federal property, or a federal agency victim).
6. **Double Jeopardy:** Under the "dual sovereignty" doctrine, a person can be prosecuted by both the state and federal government for the same conduct without violating the 5th Amendment.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"Whoever, having devised or intending to devise any scheme or artifice to defraud... transmits or causes to be transmitted by means of wire, radio, or television communication in interstate or foreign commerce... shall be fined under this title or imprisoned not more than 20 years, or both." — 18 U.S.C. § 1343.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
Solo criminal defense attorneys must be wary of "overcharging" where a simple state-level theft is turned into a 20-year federal wire fraud case due to a single email or bank transfer. Mastery of the Sentencing Guidelines is essential for effective "plea bargaining" and "mitigation" at the sentencing phase.
