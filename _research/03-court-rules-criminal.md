# Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure (FRCrP) — USA

## Source URL
https://www.uscourts.gov/rules-policies/current-rules-practice-procedure/federal-rules-criminal-procedure

## Last Verified
2026-05-18

## Authority Tier
Court Rule (Supreme Court Promulgated)

## Currency Warning
Amended annually (Dec 1). Check for recent amendments to Rule 16 (Discovery) and Rule 5 (Initial Appearance).

## Key Facts
1. **Scope:** Govern the procedure in all criminal proceedings in the U.S. district courts, courts of appeals, and the Supreme Court (Rule 1).
2. **The Complaint:** A statement of the essential facts constituting the offense charged, made under oath (Rule 3).
3. **Initial Appearance:** Must occur "without unnecessary delay" before a magistrate judge (Rule 5).
4. **Grand Jury:** Rule 6 governs the grand jury process; 16-23 jurors required for a quorum.
5. **Indictment and Information:** Rule 7; an offense punishable by death or imprisonment > 1 year must be prosecuted by indictment.
6. **Discovery:** Rule 16; the government must disclose items within its possession, custody, or control that are material to preparing the defense.
7. **Plea Agreements:** Rule 11; the court must address the defendant personally in open court and determine the plea is voluntary.
8. **Sentencing:** Rule 32; requires a presentence investigation and report.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"These rules are to be interpreted to provide for the just determination of every criminal proceeding, to secure simplicity in procedure and fairness in administration, and to eliminate unjustifiable expense and delay." — FRCrP Rule 2.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
Solo criminal defense attorneys must master Rule 16 discovery deadlines to ensure they receive all exculpatory evidence (*Brady* material). Understanding the nuances of Rule 11 is critical for advising clients on whether to accept a plea bargain versus going to trial.
