## Currency Warning
Some states (e.g., Arizona, Utah) have recently changed their rules to allow "Alternative Business Structures" (ABS) with non-lawyer ownership. This is a major shift from the traditional ABA Rule 5.4.

## Key Facts
1. **Sole Proprietorship:** Default for a single lawyer. No separate legal entity; no liability protection for personal assets. Income reported on Schedule C.
2. **PLLC (Professional Limited Liability Company):** A variation of the LLC designed for licensed professionals. Provides a "corporate veil" protecting personal assets from business debts, though not from the lawyer's own malpractice.
3. **PC (Professional Corporation) / PA (Professional Association):** Requires more formal corporate governance (minutes, bylaws). Often chosen for specific state tax benefits or "S-Corp" tax treatment.
4. **LLP (Limited Liability Partnership):** Common for small groups (2+ lawyers). Partners are generally not liable for the malpractice of other partners.
5. **Non-Lawyer Ownership (Rule 5.4):** In most states, lawyers are strictly prohibited from sharing legal fees or forming partnerships with non-lawyers.
6. **Multi-Jurisdictional Practice (MJP):** Firms practicing in multiple states must comply with the entity registration rules of each state (e.g., registering as a "Foreign LLC").
7. **S-Corp Election:** Not a separate structure, but a tax election available to LLCs and PCs to avoid double taxation and potentially lower self-employment taxes.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"A lawyer or law firm shall not share legal fees with a nonlawyer..." — ABA Model Rule 5.4(a).

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
Most modern solos choose a **PLLC with an S-Corp election**. This provides the best balance of personal asset protection and tax flexibility. However, the lawyer remains personally liable for their own professional negligence (malpractice), making robust malpractice insurance (E&O) mandatory regardless of the business structure.
