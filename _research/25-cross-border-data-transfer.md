## Currency Warning
The EU-U.S. Data Privacy Framework (DPF) is the "successor" to Privacy Shield (invalidated by *Schrems II*). It is currently under continuous legal challenge in Europe. Organizations must self-certify annually.

## Key Facts
1. **The Framework:** Allows for the "adequate" protection of personal data transferred from the EU/EEA and UK to the U.S.
2. **Self-Certification:** U.S. organizations must commit to a set of privacy principles (the "Principles") and self-certify through the Department of Commerce.
3. **Core Principles:** Notice, Choice, Accountability for Onward Transfer, Security, Data Integrity and Purpose Limitation, Access, and Recourse/Enforcement.
4. **Enforcement:** Primarily by the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) or the Department of Transportation.
5. **Redress Mechanism:** The DPF includes a "Data Protection Review Court" (DPRC) to address concerns from EU individuals regarding access to their data by U.S. intelligence agencies.
6. **Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs):** Organizations not participating in the DPF must still rely on SCCs or Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs) for transfers, which require a "Transfer Impact Assessment" (TIA).
7. **UK Extension:** The "UK Extension to the EU-U.S. DPF" allows for transfers from the UK to the U.S.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The EU-U.S. Data Privacy Framework (EU-U.S. DPF) was designed... to provide U.S. organizations with reliable mechanisms for personal data transfers to the United States from the European Union." — DataPrivacyFramework.gov.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
Solo advocates advising tech companies or service providers with European clients must ensure their clients are either DPF-certified or have valid SCCs in place. For the solo practitioner themselves, using a DPF-certified cloud provider (e.g., Microsoft, Google, AWS) simplifies their own compliance if they handle European data.
