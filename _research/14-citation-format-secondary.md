# Legal Citation Format: Secondary Sources — USA

## Last Verified

## Authority Tier
Secondary / Industry Standard (The Bluebook)

## Currency Warning
The 21st Edition (2020) is the current standard. Note that for "online-only" secondary sources (blogs, news), Rule 18 provides specific formatting that has changed significantly in the last two editions.

## Key Facts
1. **Law Review Articles:** Author, *Title*, Volume Journal Abbreviation Page (Year).
   - *Example:* Cass R. Sunstein, *Incompletely Theorized Agreements*, 108 Harv. L. Rev. 1733 (1995).
2. **Books:** Author, *Title* Pinpoint Page (Publisher/Edition Year).
   - *Example:* Richard A. Posner, *Economic Analysis of Law* 22 (9th ed. 2014).
3. **Restatements:** Restatement (Edition) of [Subject] § [Section] (Year).
   - *Example:* Restatement (Second) of Torts § 402A (Am. Law Inst. 1965).
4. **Legal Encyclopedias:** [Volume] [Encyclopedia Name] [Topic] § [Section] (Year).
   - *Example:* 88 C.J.S. *Trial* § 107 (2008).
5. **ALR Annotations:** Author, *Title*, [Volume] A.L.R.[Series] [Page] (Year).
   - *Example:* Jane Doe, *Liability for AI Malpractice*, 50 A.L.R. 7th Art. 1 (2025).
6. **Internet Sources (Rule 18):** Author, *Title*, [Main Site Name] ([Date], [Time]), [URL].
   - *Example:* SCOTUSblog, *Today's Arguments*, SCOTUSblog (May 18, 2026, 10:00 AM), https://www.scotusblog.com/...

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
While primary sources are binding, secondary sources like Restatements and Law Review articles are often used by solo advocates to argue for a "change in the law" or to fill gaps where no binding precedent exists. Precise citation of these sources adds academic weight to a practitioner's brief.
