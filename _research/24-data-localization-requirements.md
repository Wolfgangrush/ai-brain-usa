## Authority Tier
Statute (HIPAA / GLBA / FERPA) / Regulator Rule (HHS / FTC)

## Currency Warning
While the U.S. traditionally opposed data localization (favoring free flow), new Executive Orders (e.g., EO 14117) and state laws are beginning to impose restrictions on transferring bulk sensitive data to "Countries of Concern" (China, Russia, etc.).

## Key Facts
1. **No General Federal Law:** Unlike the EU (GDPR) or China (PIPL), the U.S. has no omnibus federal law requiring that data stay within U.S. borders.
2. **HIPAA (Health Data):** Does not strictly forbid storing health data abroad, but it makes the "Covered Entity" (e.g., a hospital) responsible for ensuring that the offshore provider complies with all HIPAA security and privacy rules. This is difficult to enforce, so many choose U.S.-only hosting.
3. **GLBA (Financial Data):** Focuses on the "Safeguards Rule," requiring financial institutions to protect client data. It does not mandate physical U.S. storage but requires due diligence on any foreign processor.
4. **FERPA (Education Data):** Restricts the sharing of student records. If a school uses a cloud provider, that provider must act as a "school official" under the school's direct control.
5. **ITAR (Defense Data):** The **International Traffic in Arms Regulations** (22 C.F.R. Parts 120-130) strictly requires that "technical data" related to defense items stay within the U.S. and be accessible only by "U.S. Persons."
6. **State Laws:** California (CCPA) and other states are considering "digital sovereignty" clauses that might restrict the transfer of state resident data in specific high-risk scenarios.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
Solo advocates advising clients in the defense, healthcare, or financial sectors must verify where the client's data is "at rest." If a client uses a low-cost offshore cloud provider for medical records, they are likely in technical violation of their HIPAA "Business Associate" obligations.
