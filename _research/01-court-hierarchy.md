## Currency Warning
The federal court structure remains stable under Article III of the Constitution and Title 28 of the U.S. Code.

## Key Facts
1. **Dual Court System:** The U.S. has a federal court system and independent state court systems in all 50 states and territories.
2. **Federal Three-Tier Structure:**
   - **U.S. Supreme Court:** Highest court; discretionary appellate jurisdiction via writ of certiorari.
   - **U.S. Courts of Appeals:** 13 circuits (12 regional + Federal Circuit); mandatory appellate jurisdiction over district courts.
   - **U.S. District Courts:** 94 trial courts; original jurisdiction over federal questions and diversity of citizenship cases.
3. **State Court Structure:** Most states follow a similar three-tier structure (Trial -> Intermediate Appellate -> Supreme Court), though terminology varies (e.g., NY Supreme Court is a trial court).
4. **Federal Jurisdiction:** Limited to cases involving the U.S. Constitution, federal laws, treaties, or "diversity jurisdiction" (disputes between citizens of different states with >$75,000 in controversy).
5. **State Jurisdiction:** General jurisdiction; handles most civil and criminal matters including contracts, torts, family law, and state crimes.
6. **Concurrent Jurisdiction:** Some cases can be brought in either federal or state court (e.g., federal civil rights claims).
7. **Removal:** Defendants in state court may sometimes "remove" a case to federal court if federal jurisdiction exists.
8. **Specialized Federal Courts:** Include U.S. Bankruptcy Courts, U.S. Tax Court, and U.S. Court of International Trade.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The judicial Power of the United States, shall be vested in one supreme Court, and in such inferior Courts as the Congress may from time to time ordain and establish." — U.S. Constitution, Article III, Section 1.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
Solo advocates must determine whether a case meets federal jurisdictional requirements (Federal Question or Diversity) before filing. Choosing between state and federal court involves "forum shopping" considerations such as speed of the docket, jury pools, and familiarity with local rules (LR). Failure to establish jurisdiction in federal court leads to immediate dismissal under Fed. R. Civ. P. 12(b)(1).
