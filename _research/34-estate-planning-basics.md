## Currency Warning
The federal estate tax exemption (the amount you can pass tax-free) is set to "sunset" (revert to lower levels) at the end of 2025 unless Congress acts. This makes "tax-efficient" estate planning a high priority in 2026.

## Key Facts
1. **Intestacy:** If a person dies without a will, state law determines the distribution of assets (typically to spouse and children).
2. **Last Will and Testament:** A legal document that coordinates the distribution of assets and names guardians for minor children. Must be signed and witnessed (usually 2 witnesses) to be valid.
3. **Probate:** The court-supervised process of authenticating a will and distributing assets. Can be slow and expensive, leading many to seek "probate avoidance."
4. **Revocable Living Trust:** An entity created during the grantor's lifetime to hold assets. Assets in a trust do not pass through probate, providing privacy and speed.
5. **Durable Power of Attorney (POA):** Designates an "agent" to make financial decisions if the principal becomes incapacitated.
6. **Advance Healthcare Directive:** Includes a "Healthcare Proxy" (naming someone to make medical decisions) and a "Living Will" (expressing end-of-life wishes).
7. **Beneficiary Designations:** Assets like life insurance and 401(k)s pass directly to named beneficiaries and are not governed by a will.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
Solo advocates practicing estate planning must be meticulous with "formalities" (signing, witnessing, notarizing). A single missing witness signature can invalidate an entire estate plan, leading to malpractice claims by disgruntled heirs. For the 2026 tax year, reviewing and updating "high net worth" plans for the sunsetting exemption is a major revenue driver.
