# Federal Rules of Evidence (FRE) — USA

https://www.uscourts.gov/rules-policies/current-rules-practice-procedure/federal-rules-evidence

## Last Verified
2026-05-18

## Authority Tier
Court Rule / Statute (Enacted by Congress)

## Currency Warning
Amended frequently. Recent 2023/2024 amendments focused on Rule 702 (Expert Testimony) to clarify the "gatekeeping" role of judges and Rule 106 (Remainder of or Related Writings).

## Key Facts
1. **Scope:** These rules apply to proceedings in U.S. courts (Rule 101).
2. **Relevance:** Evidence is relevant if it has any tendency to make a fact more or less probable than it would be without the evidence (Rule 401).
3. **Exclusion (Rule 403):** The court may exclude relevant evidence if its probative value is substantially outweighed by a danger of unfair prejudice, confusing the issues, misleading the jury, etc.
4. **Hearsay (Rule 801):** A statement that (1) the declarant does not make while testifying at the current trial or hearing; and (2) a party offers in evidence to prove the truth of the matter asserted in the statement. Generally inadmissible (Rule 802).
5. **Hearsay Exceptions:** Numerous exceptions exist, including "Present Sense Impression," "Excited Utterance," and "Records of a Regularly Conducted Activity" (Business Records exception — Rule 803(6)).
6. **Expert Testimony (Rule 702):** The *Daubert* standard; the proponent must demonstrate that the expert's knowledge will help the trier of fact and the testimony is based on sufficient facts or data.
7. **Privileges:** Rule 501; in federal question cases, federal common law governs privileges (e.g., Attorney-Client Privilege).
8. **Authentication:** Rule 901; the proponent must produce evidence sufficient to support a finding that the item is what the proponent claims it is.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The court must decide any preliminary question about whether a witness is qualified, a privilege exists, or evidence is admissible." — FRE Rule 104(a).

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
Solo litigators must be "experts in the rules of evidence" to win at trial. Success often depends on winning "motions in limine" before trial to exclude the opponent's damaging evidence under Rule 403 or Rule 802. Documenting the "chain of custody" and "authentication" of digital evidence is a modern requirement.
