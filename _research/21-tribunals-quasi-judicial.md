## Currency Warning
The Supreme Court's 2024 decision in *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* (overturning *Chevron* deference) has significantly changed how courts review agency decisions, making it easier to challenge "quasi-judicial" agency interpretations of statutes.

## Key Facts
1. **Administrative Procedure Act (APA):** The "constitution" for federal agencies; governs how they make rules and adjudicate disputes.
2. **EEOC (Equal Employment Opportunity Commission):** Handles claims of workplace discrimination. Most employees must "exhaust administrative remedies" by filing a charge with the EEOC before they can sue in court.
3. **MSPB (Merit Systems Protection Board):** Protects the "merit system" for federal employees; handles appeals of disciplinary actions and whistleblower claims.
4. **Administrative Law Judges (ALJs):** Specialized judges within an agency who hear cases. Their decisions are usually appealable to the agency's Board and then to a U.S. Court of Appeals.
5. **NLRB (National Labor Relations Board):** Adjudicates "Unfair Labor Practice" (ULP) charges related to union activity and collective bargaining.
6. **Standard of Review:** Under the APA, agency adjudications are typically reviewed under the "arbitrary and capricious" or "substantial evidence" standard.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The reviewing court shall... hold unlawful and set aside agency action, findings, and conclusions found to be— (A) arbitrary, capricious, an abuse of discretion, or otherwise not in accordance with law..." — 5 U.S.C. § 706.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
Solo practitioners specializing in Employment Law or Social Security Disability (SSDI) spend the majority of their time in these "quasi-judicial" forums rather than in traditional courts. Understanding the "Administrative Record" is critical, as new evidence usually cannot be introduced on appeal to a federal court.
