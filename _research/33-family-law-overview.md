## Currency Warning
The legal recognition of "de facto parents" and the rights of non-biological parents are evolving rapidly through state court decisions. No-fault divorce is the standard, but some states (e.g., Texas) still maintain "fault" grounds which can impact alimony.

## Key Facts
1. **Jurisdiction:** Family law is almost exclusively a matter of state law. Federal courts generally lack jurisdiction over divorce, alimony, and child custody (*Ankenbrandt v. Richards*).
2. **Marriage:** Governed by state requirements (age, capacity, license). Same-sex marriage is constitutionally protected nationwide (*Obergefell v. Hodges*).
3. **No-Fault Divorce:** Allows for dissolution of marriage based on "irreconcilable differences" or "irretrievable breakdown" without proving wrongdoing.
4. **Child Custody:** Determined based on the "Best Interests of the Child" standard. Most states favor "joint legal custody" (shared decision-making).
5. **Child Support:** Calculated using state-specific "guidelines" (often a formula based on parental income and time spent with the child).
6. **Property Division:**
   - **Community Property (e.g., CA, TX):** 50/50 split of all assets acquired during the marriage.
   - **Equitable Distribution (Most states):** "Fair" but not necessarily equal split based on various factors (contribution, length of marriage, future earning capacity).
7. **Prenuptial Agreements:** Valid if they are in writing, involve full financial disclosure, and are not unconscionable at the time of enforcement.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
Family law is the "bread and butter" of many solo practices. It requires not only legal knowledge but high emotional intelligence (EQ). Solos must be wary of "unbundled legal services" (limited scope representation) in family law, which is a growing trend to increase access for those who cannot afford full representation.
