## Currency Warning
The CCPA was significantly expanded by the California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA) in 2020. Most substantive provisions took effect January 1, 2023. This is currently the most comprehensive state privacy law in the U.S. and often serves as a de facto national standard.

## Key Facts
1. **Scope:** Applies to for-profit businesses doing business in California that meet certain thresholds (e.g., $25M gross revenue, or processing data of 50k/100k consumers).
2. **Consumer Rights:**
   - **Right to Know:** What personal information is collected.
   - **Right to Delete:** Personal information collected from the consumer.
   - **Right to Opt-Out:** Sale or sharing of personal information.
   - **Right to Correct:** Inaccurate personal information.
   - **Right to Limit:** Use and disclosure of sensitive personal information.
3. **Sensitive Personal Information:** Includes SSN, driver's license, precise geolocation, racial/ethnic origin, etc.
4. **Data Minimization:** Businesses must not collect more personal information than is necessary and proportionate to achieve the purpose for which it was collected.
5. **Private Right of Action:** Limited to data breaches resulting from a business's failure to maintain reasonable security procedures (Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.150). Statutory damages: $100–$750 per consumer per incident.
6. **Enforcement:** Primarily by the California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA) and the Attorney General.
7. **Notice at Collection:** Businesses must inform consumers at or before the point of collection about the categories of personal information to be collected.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"A consumer shall have the right to request that a business that collects personal information about the consumer disclose to the consumer the categories and specific pieces of personal information the business has collected." — Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.100(a).

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
Solo advocates representing small businesses must determine if their clients meet the CCPA's thresholds. For litigation, the private right of action for data breaches provides a powerful tool for class actions, but requires a "notice and cure" period in some circumstances. Solo practitioners themselves must ensure their firm's website and intake processes comply if they meet the thresholds or handle significant California resident data.
