## Currency Warning
The legal licensing landscape is in a state of flux. The **Uniform Bar Exam (UBE)** is being replaced by the **NextGen Bar Exam**, which launches in July 2026. Some states (e.g., Oregon, Washington) are also introducing "Alternative Pathways" to licensure like supervised practice (Apprenticeships).

## Key Facts
1. **Education:** Most states require a Juris Doctor (J.D.) from an ABA-accredited law school. (Exceptions: California allows "Reading the Law" or non-ABA schools).
2. **The Bar Exam:**
   - **MBE (Multistate Bar Exam):** 200 multiple-choice questions on 7 core subjects.
   - **MEE (Multistate Essay Exam):** 6 essays on a broader range of subjects.
   - **MPT (Multistate Performance Test):** 2 tasks simulating "real-world" lawyering (memos, briefs).
3. **UBE (Uniform Bar Exam):** A standardized score that is portable across 40+ jurisdictions.
4. **NextGen Bar Exam (July 2026):** Focuses more on "lawyering skills" (research, drafting, negotiation) and less on memorization of niche legal doctrines.
5. **Character and Fitness:** A rigorous background check into the applicant's past (criminal record, financial responsibility, mental health).
6. **MPRE (Multistate Professional Responsibility Examination):** A separate ethics exam required by almost all jurisdictions.
7. **Admission on Motion (Reciprocity):** After practicing for a certain number of years (typically 5), a lawyer may be admitted to another state's bar without taking the exam, provided the states have a reciprocal agreement.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
Solo advocates wishing to expand their practice to other states should prioritize taking the UBE or checking the "Admission on Motion" rules of their target state. The transition to the NextGen Bar Exam in 2026 will change how law students are trained and how solos might recruit junior associates.
