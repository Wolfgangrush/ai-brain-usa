# Motion for TRO and Preliminary Injunction (Rule 65)

## Source URLs
- [Fed. R. Civ. P. 65](https://www.law.cornell.edu/rules/frcp/rule_65)
- [*Winter v. NRDC*, 555 U.S. 7 (2008)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/555/7/)

## Last Verified
2026-05-29

## Authority Tier
Primary — FRCP Rule 65 + *Winter* four-factor test.

## Currency Warning
TRO without notice — Rule 65(b) — requires specific facts of immediate and irreparable injury before adverse party can be heard + certification of efforts to give notice. TRO duration: not more than 14 days; extendable once for good cause (Rule 65(b)(2)). Preliminary injunction requires notice and opportunity to be heard. *Trademark Modernization Act of 2020* — for trademark cases, rebuttable presumption of irreparable harm on showing likelihood of success (15 U.S.C. § 1116(a)).

## Template Type
Emergency Motion + Memorandum + Declarations + Proposed Order.

## Structural Skeleton
1. **Notice of Emergency Motion** — relief sought (TRO + Preliminary Injunction); hearing time requested.
2. **Memorandum in Support**:
   a. **Introduction** — emergency posture; specific irreparable harm.
   b. **Factual Background** — supported by declarations.
   c. **Legal Standard** — Rule 65 + Winter four factors:
      - **Likelihood of success on the merits.**
      - **Irreparable harm absent relief.**
      - **Balance of equities tipping in movant's favor.**
      - **Public interest favoring relief.**
   d. **Argument applying each factor**:
      - **Likelihood of success** — element-by-element analysis with evidence cites.
      - **Irreparable harm** — distinguish from monetary harm; demonstrate inadequacy of post-judgment remedy; cite specific facts (loss of goodwill / trade secrets / privacy).
      - **Balance of equities** — relative hardships.
      - **Public interest** — particularly important for government defendants and matters with broad consumer impact.
3. **Declarations / Verifications** under 28 U.S.C. § 1746 — supporting each factual allegation.
4. **Proposed Order** (Rule 65(d)):
   - Specific in terms; describes act(s) restrained; binds parties + their officers, agents, employees, attorneys, and persons in active concert who receive actual notice.
5. **Security / Bond** — Rule 65(c): movant generally must give security in such sum as the court considers proper; nominal bond often acceptable in non-commercial cases or where harm to opposing party speculative.
6. **Certificate of Counsel's Efforts to Notify** (for TRO without notice — Rule 65(b)(1)(B)).
7. **Hearing Request** — TRO often heard ex parte; PI requires notice + briefing schedule.

## Drafting Conventions
- *Winter* rejects "possibility-of-irreparable-harm" standard — requires "likely to suffer irreparable harm."
- *Munaf v. Geren*, 553 U.S. 674 (2008), confirms PI is "extraordinary remedy."
- For First Amendment cases, loss of First Amendment freedoms unquestionably constitutes irreparable injury (*Elrod v. Burns*, 427 U.S. 347 (1976)).
- Bond requirement: courts have discretion; nominal $100 bond not unusual for public-interest cases.
- Trade-secret cases: pair with affidavit identifying with specificity what trade-secret information is at risk.
- Trademark cases (post-TMA 2020): cite 15 U.S.C. § 1116(a) presumption.

## Fair-Use Excerpt
> "The court may issue a temporary restraining order without written or oral notice to the adverse party or its attorney only if: (A) specific facts in an affidavit or a verified complaint clearly show that immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result to the movant before the adverse party can be heard in opposition; and (B) the movant's attorney certifies in writing any efforts made to give notice and the reasons why it should not be required." — Fed. R. Civ. P. 65(b)(1).

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
Highest-stakes interim motion practice. Pair with verified complaint, supporting declarations, security/bond proposal, and a meet-and-confer record (where time permits notice).
