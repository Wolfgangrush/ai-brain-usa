# Motion for Judgment of Acquittal (FRCrP Rule 29)

## Source URLs
- [Fed. R. Crim. P. 29](https://www.law.cornell.edu/rules/frcrmp/rule_29)
- [*Jackson v. Virginia*, 443 U.S. 307 (1979)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/443/307/)

## Last Verified
2026-05-29

## Authority Tier
Primary — FRCrP Rule 29 + Jackson sufficiency standard.

## Currency Warning
Rule 29 motions preserve sufficiency-of-the-evidence claims on appeal. Standard: viewing evidence in light most favorable to government, no rational trier of fact could find each element beyond reasonable doubt.

## Template Type
Mid-Trial / Post-Verdict Criminal Motion.

## Structural Skeleton
1. **Caption** — criminal case caption + "DEFENDANT'S MOTION FOR JUDGMENT OF ACQUITTAL UNDER FEDERAL RULE OF CRIMINAL PROCEDURE 29."
2. **Procedural Posture** — close of government's evidence (Rule 29(a)) / close of all evidence (Rule 29(a)) / post-verdict within 14 days (Rule 29(c)).
3. **Charges** — list each count.
4. **Argument** — count-by-count:
   - State each element of the offense.
   - Identify evidence presented by government on each element.
   - Show that evidence, viewed in light most favorable to government, is insufficient to prove that element beyond a reasonable doubt.
   - Address any sufficiency-saving inferences and rebut.
5. **Relief Requested** — judgment of acquittal on specified counts.
6. **Reservation** — even if denied, preserves sufficiency challenge for appeal.

## Drafting Conventions
- Timing — Rule 29(a) at close of government case or close of all evidence; Rule 29(c) post-verdict within 14 days (or extended period set before deadline expires).
- Standard reviewed de novo on appeal but with same deferential standard.
- For each element: identify what the government must prove; identify what it actually proved; argue gap.
- *Burks v. United States*, 437 U.S. 1 (1978): reversal for insufficient evidence bars retrial on Double Jeopardy.
- *United States v. Powell*, 469 U.S. 57 (1984): inconsistent verdicts not basis for Rule 29.

## Fair-Use Excerpt
> "After the government closes its evidence or after the close of all the evidence, the court on the defendant's motion must enter a judgment of acquittal of any offense for which the evidence is insufficient to sustain a conviction. The court may on its own consider whether the evidence is insufficient to sustain a conviction." — Fed. R. Crim. P. 29(a).

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
Essential preservation motion in every federal criminal trial — preserves the strongest appellate ground (sufficiency).
