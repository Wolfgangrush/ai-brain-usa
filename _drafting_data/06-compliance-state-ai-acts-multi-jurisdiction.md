## Structural Skeleton
1. **Coverage Matrix** — for each act:
   - Effective date.
   - Geographic trigger (consumer residency / business presence).
   - Covered entity (developer · deployer · both).
   - Covered AI systems (high-risk · generative · consumer-facing).
   - Threshold (revenue / users / data scale).
2. **Definitions Cross-Walk** — "high-risk AI system" (CO) vs. "AI" (UT) vs. "artificial intelligence" (TX TRAIGA) vs. "generative artificial intelligence" (CA AB 2013).
3. **Affirmative Obligations** by jurisdiction:
   - **Colorado** — algorithmic discrimination avoidance · impact assessments · risk management programme · consumer notice · right to appeal/correct.
   - **Texas TRAIGA** — prohibited uses (social scoring / behavioural manipulation / certain biometric categorization / certain emotion recognition) · government-AI obligations · pre-deployment risk identification.
   - **Utah** — disclosure obligation when AI used in consumer-facing interaction (especially regulated occupations).
   - **California AB 2013** — public training-data disclosure on developer website for GenAI models made available 1 Jan 2022 onward.
   - **California SB 942** — AI Transparency Act — content provenance + manifest disclosure for covered generative providers (>1M monthly users in California).
4. **Enforcement Authority** — state AG (CO, TX, UT, CA) + private right of action analysis (most do NOT grant PROA).
5. **Penalties Matrix** — civil penalty per violation + injunctive relief.
6. **Federal Preemption Watch** — track AI Preemption Act (HR 5099 100-MW Innovation Act) + related bills.
7. **Common-control template** — single internal compliance programme covering the strictest of the overlapping obligations.

## Drafting Conventions
- Build the compliance programme to the strictest applicable state's obligation, then document jurisdiction-specific carve-outs.
- Distinguish "developer" obligations from "deployer" obligations — Colorado is the clearest split.
- Track preemption — if federal AI omnibus passes preempting state law, retire state-specific controls.

## Fair-Use Excerpt
> "A developer of a high-risk artificial intelligence system shall use reasonable care to protect consumers from any known or reasonably foreseeable risks of algorithmic discrimination arising from the intended and contracted uses of the high-risk artificial intelligence system." — Colorado AI Act § 6-1-1702(1).
