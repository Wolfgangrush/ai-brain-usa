## Section-by-Section Digest

### ERISA § 1104(a)(1) — Fiduciary Duties

**Plain-English summary:** A fiduciary shall discharge duties with respect to the plan solely in the interest of the participants and beneficiaries and: (A) for the exclusive purpose of providing benefits and defraying reasonable administrative expenses; (B) with the care, skill, prudence, and diligence under the circumstances then prevailing that a prudent person acting in a like capacity and familiar with such matters would use; (C) by diversifying investments to minimize the risk of large losses; (D) in accordance with the plan documents and instruments.

**Statutory text (verbatim, partial):**
> Subject to sections 1103(c) and (d), 1342, and 1344 of this title, a fiduciary shall discharge his duties with respect to a plan solely in the interest of the participants and beneficiaries and — (A) for the exclusive purpose of: (i) providing benefits to participants and their beneficiaries; and (ii) defraying reasonable expenses of administering the plan; (B) with the care, skill, prudence, and diligence under the circumstances then prevailing that a prudent man acting in a like capacity and familiar with such matters would use in the conduct of an enterprise of a like character and with like aims; (C) by diversifying the investments of the plan so as to minimize the risk of large losses, unless under the circumstances it is clearly not prudent to do so; and (D) in accordance with the documents and instruments governing the plan…

**Citation format:** 29 U.S.C. § 1104(a)(1).

**Practical use:** Core of every fiduciary-breach claim. *Hughes v. Northwestern University*, 595 U.S. 170 (2022), held categorical defenses (offering varied options) do not defeat duty-of-prudence claims based on imprudent menu items.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

### ERISA § 1132 — Civil Enforcement

**Plain-English summary:** Civil action may be brought by participants, beneficiaries, fiduciaries, and the Secretary of Labor for various forms of relief, including benefits due (§ 1132(a)(1)(B)), fiduciary breach (§ 1132(a)(2) — recovery to plan; (a)(3) — equitable relief), and interference with rights (§ 1132(a)(3)).

**Statutory text (verbatim, partial):**
> A civil action may be brought — (1) by a participant or beneficiary — (A) for the relief provided for in subsection (c) of this section, or (B) to recover benefits due to him under the terms of his plan, to enforce his rights under the terms of the plan, or to clarify his rights to future benefits under the terms of the plan; (2) by the Secretary, or by a participant, beneficiary or fiduciary for appropriate relief under section 1109 of this title…

**Citation format:** 29 U.S.C. § 1132(a).

**Practical use:** Standard of review for § 1132(a)(1)(B) benefits denials: de novo unless plan grants discretion (*Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. v. Bruch*, 489 U.S. 101 (1989)). Equitable relief under § 1132(a)(3) — narrow per *Mertens v. Hewitt Assocs.*, 508 U.S. 248 (1993), and *Montanile v. Bd. of Trs.*, 577 U.S. 136 (2016).

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

### ERISA § 1144 — Preemption

**Plain-English summary:** ERISA shall supersede any and all State laws insofar as they may now or hereafter relate to any employee benefit plan covered by ERISA. The "savings clause" preserves state laws regulating insurance, banking, or securities; the "deemer clause" prevents states from regulating self-funded plans as insurers.

**Citation format:** 29 U.S.C. § 1144(a)–(b).

**Practical use:** *Rutledge v. Pharmaceutical Care Management Ass'n*, 592 U.S. 80 (2020), narrowed preemption scope — state laws of general applicability with only incidental cost effects survive.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### FLSA § 206(a) — Minimum Wage

**Plain-English summary:** Every employer shall pay to each of his employees who in any workweek is engaged in commerce or in the production of goods for commerce, or is employed in an enterprise engaged in commerce or in the production of goods for commerce, wages at the following rate: not less than $7.25 an hour beginning 24 July 2009.

**Citation format:** 29 U.S.C. § 206(a)(1).

**Practical use:** Federal floor only — state and municipal minimum wages may be higher. Tipped-wage workers subject to § 203(m) tip credit. Equal Pay Act at § 206(d) prohibits sex-based wage differentials for equal work.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### FLSA § 207(a) — Overtime

**Plain-English summary:** No employer shall employ any of his employees who in any workweek is engaged in commerce or in the production of goods for commerce, or is employed in an enterprise engaged in commerce or in the production of goods for commerce, for a workweek longer than forty hours unless such employee receives compensation for his employment in excess of forty hours at a rate not less than one and one-half times the regular rate at which he is employed.

**Statutory text (verbatim, partial):**
> Except as otherwise provided in this section, no employer shall employ any of his employees who in any workweek is engaged in commerce or in the production of goods for commerce, or is employed in an enterprise engaged in commerce or in the production of goods for commerce, for a workweek longer than forty hours unless such employee receives compensation for his employment in excess of the hours above specified at a rate not less than one and one-half times the regular rate at which he is employed.

**Citation format:** 29 U.S.C. § 207(a)(1).

**Practical use:** "Regular rate" calculation under § 207(e) — workhorse of FLSA litigation. *Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Bouaphakeo*, 577 U.S. 442 (2016), permits representative evidence in collective actions.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### FLSA § 213 — White-Collar Exemptions

**Plain-English summary:** Section 213(a)(1) exempts from §§ 206 and 207 any employee employed in a bona fide executive, administrative, or professional capacity (or in the capacity of outside salesman). Implementing regulations at 29 C.F.R. Part 541 require: (i) salary basis, (ii) salary level (currently subject to litigation following *Texas v. U.S. Dep't of Labor* (2024)), (iii) job-duties test.

**Citation format:** 29 U.S.C. § 213(a)(1).

**Practical use:** *Helix Energy Solutions Group v. Hewitt*, 598 U.S. 39 (2023) — even highly compensated employees must satisfy the salary-basis test; day-rate compensation does not qualify.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### FLSA § 216(b) — Private Cause of Action

**Plain-English summary:** Any employer who violates §§ 206 or 207 shall be liable to the affected employees for unpaid wages or unpaid overtime compensation, as the case may be, plus an additional equal amount as liquidated damages. Action may be maintained on behalf of similarly situated employees who give written consent to join (opt-in collective action).

**Citation format:** 29 U.S.C. § 216(b).

**Practical use:** Two-year statute of limitations (three for willful violations under § 255(a)). *Bristol-Myers Squibb Co. v. Superior Ct.*, 582 U.S. 255 (2017), affects personal-jurisdiction analysis for non-resident opt-ins.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

## Drafting Hooks
- Every ERISA benefits-denial complaint identifies the § 1132(a)(1)(B) or (a)(3) hook and the standard-of-review framework.
- Every collective-action complaint addresses notice and conditional certification under § 216(b).
- Every misclassification analysis runs through § 213(a)(1) duties and salary tests.
