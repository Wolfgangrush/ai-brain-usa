## Section-by-Section Digest

### Lanham Act § 1125(a) — False Designation of Origin

**Plain-English summary:** A person who uses in commerce any word, term, name, symbol, or device, or any false designation of origin, that is likely to cause confusion as to affiliation, connection, or association, or as to the origin, sponsorship, or approval of goods or services, shall be liable in a civil action.

**Statutory text (verbatim, partial):**
> Any person who, on or in connection with any goods or services… uses in commerce any word, term, name, symbol, or device, or any combination thereof, or any false designation of origin, false or misleading description of fact, or false or misleading representation of fact, which — (A) is likely to cause confusion, or to cause mistake, or to deceive as to the affiliation, connection, or association of such person with another person, or as to the origin, sponsorship, or approval of his or her goods, services, or commercial activities by another person… shall be liable in a civil action by any person who believes that he or she is or is likely to be damaged by such act.

**Citation format:** 15 U.S.C. § 1125(a)(1)(A).

**Practical use:** Vehicle for unregistered-mark and trade-dress claims. Post-*Jack Daniel's*, parody/expressive-use defenses are narrowed where the defendant uses the mark *as a mark*.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

### Copyright Act § 106 — Exclusive Rights in Copyrighted Works

**Plain-English summary:** Subject to §§ 107–122, the owner of copyright has the exclusive rights to (1) reproduce, (2) prepare derivative works, (3) distribute copies, (4) perform publicly, (5) display publicly, and (6) perform sound recordings by digital audio transmission.

**Statutory text (verbatim, partial):**
> Subject to sections 107 through 122, the owner of copyright under this title has the exclusive rights to do and to authorize any of the following: (1) to reproduce the copyrighted work in copies or phonorecords; (2) to prepare derivative works based upon the copyrighted work; (3) to distribute copies or phonorecords of the copyrighted work to the public by sale or other transfer of ownership, or by rental, lease, or lending; (4) in the case of literary, musical, dramatic, and choreographic works… to perform the copyrighted work publicly; (5) in the case of literary, musical, dramatic, and choreographic works, pantomimes, and pictorial, graphic, or sculptural works… to display the copyrighted work publicly; and (6) in the case of sound recordings, to perform the copyrighted work publicly by means of a digital audio transmission.

**Citation format:** 17 U.S.C. § 106.

**Practical use:** Foundation of every copyright infringement complaint — plaintiff must identify which § 106 rights were violated.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

### Copyright Act § 107 — Fair Use

**Plain-English summary:** Fair use of a copyrighted work is not infringement. The four factors are: (1) purpose and character of the use (including commercial nature and transformative character), (2) nature of the copyrighted work, (3) amount and substantiality of the portion used in relation to the whole, (4) effect on the potential market.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> Notwithstanding the provisions of sections 106 and 106A, the fair use of a copyrighted work, including such use by reproduction in copies or phonorecords or by any other means specified by that section, for purposes such as criticism, comment, news reporting, teaching (including multiple copies for classroom use), scholarship, or research, is not an infringement of copyright. In determining whether the use made of a work in any particular case is a fair use the factors to be considered shall include — (1) the purpose and character of the use, including whether such use is of a commercial nature or is for nonprofit educational purposes; (2) the nature of the copyrighted work; (3) the amount and substantiality of the portion used in relation to the copyrighted work as a whole; and (4) the effect of the use upon the potential market for or value of the copyrighted work.

**Citation format:** 17 U.S.C. § 107.

**Practical use:** *Andy Warhol Found. v. Goldsmith*, 598 U.S. 508 (2023), refocused factor 1 on whether the use shares the same commercial purpose as the original — substantial implications for generative-AI training corpora.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

### Copyright Act § 512 — DMCA Safe Harbor (Online Service Providers)

**Plain-English summary:** Section 512 creates four safe harbors for online service providers (transitory communications · system caching · user-stored material · information location tools). The most-litigated is § 512(c) — user-stored material — requiring notice-and-takedown compliance.

**Citation format:** 17 U.S.C. § 512(c).

**Practical use:** Designation of a DMCA agent at the Copyright Office is jurisdictional for the safe harbor. *Lenz v. Universal Music Corp.*, 815 F.3d 1145 (9th Cir. 2016) — fair use must be considered before issuing a § 512(c)(3) takedown notice.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Patent Act § 101 — Patentable Subject Matter

**Plain-English summary:** Whoever invents or discovers any new and useful process, machine, manufacture, or composition of matter, or any new and useful improvement thereof, may obtain a patent therefor, subject to the conditions and requirements of this title.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> Whoever invents or discovers any new and useful process, machine, manufacture, or composition of matter, or any new and useful improvement thereof, may obtain a patent therefor, subject to the conditions and requirements of this title.

**Citation format:** 35 U.S.C. § 101.

**Practical use:** Read with the two-step *Alice/Mayo* framework — *Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank Int'l*, 573 U.S. 208 (2014); *Mayo Collaborative Servs. v. Prometheus Labs.*, 566 U.S. 66 (2012). Step 1: is the claim directed to a patent-ineligible concept (abstract idea, law of nature, natural phenomenon)? Step 2: does it recite an "inventive concept" sufficient to transform the claim?

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Patent Act § 271 — Infringement

**Plain-English summary:** Whoever without authority makes, uses, offers to sell, or sells any patented invention within the United States, or imports into the United States any patented invention during the term of the patent, infringes the patent.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> (a) Except as otherwise provided in this title, whoever without authority makes, uses, offers to sell, or sells any patented invention, within the United States or imports into the United States any patented invention during the term of the patent therefor, infringes the patent.

**Citation format:** 35 U.S.C. § 271(a).

**Practical use:** Direct infringement framework. Active inducement under § 271(b) and contributory infringement under § 271(c) supplement direct claims.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

## Drafting Hooks
- Every trademark complaint identifies likelihood-of-confusion factors (DuPont / Polaroid / Sleekcraft depending on circuit).
- Every copyright complaint identifies the § 106 rights infringed and pleads § 411 registration prerequisite.
- Every patent complaint maps each accused product element-by-element to the asserted claims.
- Every fair-use brief tracks the four § 107 factors post-*Warhol*.
