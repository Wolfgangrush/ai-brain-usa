# Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA)

## Citation Block
- **Statute:** 18 U.S.C. § 1030 (criminal); civil cause of action at 18 U.S.C. § 1030(g).
- **Official URL:** [https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title18-section1030&edition=prelim](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title18-section1030&edition=prelim)
- **Bluebook Citation:** *18 U.S.C. § 1030(a)(N)*.

## Authority Tier
Tier 1 — Cyber/Commercial-Litigation Backbone.

## Currency Block
- **Original Enactment:** 1986 (Pub. L. 99-474).
- **Major Amendments:** Identity Theft and Assumption Deterrence Act 1998; USA PATRIOT Act 2001; Identity Theft Enforcement and Restitution Act 2008; CFAA Amendments Act (proposed multiple times; never enacted).
- **Doctrinal currents:** *Van Buren v. United States*, 593 U.S. 374 (2021) — narrowed "exceeds authorized access" to *gates-up-or-down* (whether the user accessed a portion of the system off-limits to them), not the *purpose* for which information was used.

## Scope of Digest
- **Subsections covered:** § 1030(a)(1)–(7) (criminal prohibitions), § 1030(c) (penalties), § 1030(e) (definitions), § 1030(g) (civil remedy).

## Section-by-Section Digest

### § 1030(a)(2) — Unauthorized Access to Information

**Plain-English summary:** Prohibits intentionally accessing a computer without authorization or exceeding authorized access and thereby obtaining (A) information contained in a financial record of a financial institution, (B) information from any department or agency of the United States, or (C) information from any protected computer.

**Statutory text (verbatim, partial):**
> Whoever — (2) intentionally accesses a computer without authorization or exceeds authorized access, and thereby obtains — (A) information contained in a financial record of a financial institution, or of a card issuer…; (B) information from any department or agency of the United States; or (C) information from any protected computer; shall be punished as provided in subsection (c) of this section.

**Citation format:** 18 U.S.C. § 1030(a)(2).

**Practical use:** Workhorse subsection for civil claims by employers against former employees, and by online platforms against scrapers. Post-*Van Buren*, "exceeds authorized access" requires showing the defendant accessed files, folders, or databases off-limits to them.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### § 1030(a)(5) — Damage and Loss to Protected Computers

**Plain-English summary:** Three subparagraphs: (A) knowingly transmitting code resulting in unauthorized damage; (B) intentionally accessing without authorization and recklessly causing damage; (C) intentionally accessing without authorization and causing damage and loss.

**Statutory text (verbatim, partial):**
> Whoever — (5)(A) knowingly causes the transmission of a program, information, code, or command, and as a result of such conduct, intentionally causes damage without authorization, to a protected computer; (B) intentionally accesses a protected computer without authorization, and as a result of such conduct, recklessly causes damage; or (C) intentionally accesses a protected computer without authorization, and as a result of such conduct, causes damage and loss…

**Citation format:** 18 U.S.C. § 1030(a)(5)(A)–(C).

**Practical use:** Anchor for ransomware and intrusion litigation.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### § 1030(g) — Civil Remedy

**Plain-English summary:** Any person who suffers damage or loss by reason of a violation of this section may maintain a civil action against the violator to obtain compensatory damages and injunctive or other equitable relief — provided the conduct involves at least one of five factors, including loss aggregating at least $5,000 in any 1-year period.

**Statutory text (verbatim, partial):**
> Any person who suffers damage or loss by reason of a violation of this section may maintain a civil action against the violator to obtain compensatory damages and injunctive relief or other equitable relief. A civil action for a violation of this section may be brought only if the conduct involves 1 of the factors set forth in subclauses (I), (II), (III), (IV), or (V) of subsection (c)(4)(A)(i)…

**Citation format:** 18 U.S.C. § 1030(g).

**Practical use:** $5,000 loss threshold under (c)(4)(A)(i)(I) is the most-litigated jurisdictional hook. Statute of limitations: two years from discovery.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### § 1030(e)(11) — "Loss" Definition

**Plain-English summary:** "Loss" means any reasonable cost to any victim, including the cost of responding to an offense, conducting a damage assessment, and restoring data, programs, system, or information to its condition prior to the offense, and any revenue lost, cost incurred, or other consequential damages incurred because of interruption of service.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> The term "loss" means any reasonable cost to any victim, including the cost of responding to an offense, conducting a damage assessment, and restoring the data, program, system, or information to its condition prior to the offense, and any revenue lost, cost incurred, or other consequential damages incurred because of interruption of service.

**Citation format:** 18 U.S.C. § 1030(e)(11).

**Practical use:** Plaintiffs typically aggregate forensic costs, IT response time, and business disruption damages to meet the $5,000 threshold.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

## Drafting Hooks
- Every CFAA civil complaint pleads the $5,000 loss threshold with itemized damages.
- Every post-*Van Buren* "exceeds authorized access" claim alleges off-limits files/folders, not improper purpose.
- Pair with Defend Trade Secrets Act (18 U.S.C. § 1836) for employee-departure trade-secret cases.
