## Section-by-Section Digest

### Rule 3(a) — Filing the Notice of Appeal

**Plain-English summary:** An appeal permitted by law as of right from a district court to a court of appeals may be taken only by filing a notice of appeal with the district clerk within the time allowed by Rule 4.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> An appeal permitted by law as of right from a district court to a court of appeals may be taken only by filing a notice of appeal with the district clerk within the time allowed by Rule 4. At the time of filing, the appellant must furnish the clerk with enough copies of the notice to enable the clerk to comply with Rule 3(d).

**Citation format:** Fed. R. App. P. 3(a)(1).

**Practical use:** Filing notice of appeal is jurisdictional — *Bowles v. Russell*, 551 U.S. 205 (2007).

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 4(a)(1) — Appeal in a Civil Case — Time

**Plain-English summary:** In a civil case, the notice of appeal must be filed with the district clerk within 30 days after entry of the judgment or order appealed from. When the United States or its officer or agency is a party, the time is 60 days.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> In a civil case, except as provided in Rules 4(a)(1)(B), 4(a)(4), and 4(c), the notice of appeal required by Rule 3 must be filed with the district clerk within 30 days after entry of the judgment or order appealed from. When the United States, its officer, or agency is a party, the notice may be filed by any party within 60 days after entry of the judgment or order appealed from.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. App. P. 4(a)(1)(A)–(B).

**Practical use:** Time limit is mandatory and jurisdictional (*Bowles*). Tolled by post-judgment motions enumerated in Rule 4(a)(4).

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 8 — Stay or Injunction Pending Appeal

**Plain-English summary:** A party must ordinarily move first in the district court for a stay of judgment or order; if denied, the motion may be made in the court of appeals.

**Statutory text (verbatim, partial):**
> A party must ordinarily move first in the district court for the following relief: (A) a stay of the judgment or order of a district court pending appeal; (B) approval of a supersedeas bond; or (C) an order suspending, modifying, restoring, or granting an injunction while an appeal is pending.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. App. P. 8(a)(1).

**Practical use:** Applies the *Nken v. Holder*, 556 U.S. 418 (2009), four-factor stay test (likelihood of success · irreparable injury · balance of equities · public interest).

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 28(a) — Appellant's Brief

**Plain-English summary:** The appellant's brief must contain (1) corporate disclosure statement, (2) table of contents, (3) table of authorities, (4) jurisdictional statement, (5) statement of issues, (6) statement of the case, (7) summary of argument, (8) argument with applicable standard of review, (9) conclusion, (10) certificate of compliance, (11) signature.

**Statutory text (verbatim, partial):**
> The appellant's brief must contain, under appropriate headings and in the order indicated: (1) a corporate disclosure statement if required by Rule 26.1; (2) a table of contents…; (3) a table of authorities…; (4) a jurisdictional statement…; (5) a statement of the issues presented for review; (6) a concise statement of the case setting out the facts relevant to the issues submitted for review…; (7) a summary of the argument…; (8) the argument…; (9) a short conclusion stating the precise relief sought…

**Citation format:** Fed. R. App. P. 28(a).

**Practical use:** Mandatory brief skeleton. Failure to include any required element risks a Rule 28 deficiency notice.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 32(a)(7) — Length Limits

**Plain-English summary:** A principal brief may not exceed 13,000 words; a reply brief 6,500 words. Word counts use the certificate of compliance under Rule 32(g).

**Statutory text (verbatim, partial):**
> A principal brief may contain no more than 13,000 words. A reply brief may contain no more than half of the type-volume limitation specified for a principal brief.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. App. P. 32(a)(7)(B).

**Practical use:** Watch circuit-specific local rules — some circuits have shorter limits. Always file Rule 32(g) certificate of compliance.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 35 — En Banc Determination

**Plain-English summary:** An en banc hearing or rehearing is not favored and will not ordinarily be ordered unless: (1) en banc consideration is necessary to secure or maintain uniformity of decisions, or (2) the proceeding involves a question of exceptional importance.

**Statutory text (verbatim, partial):**
> An en banc hearing or rehearing is not favored and ordinarily will not be ordered unless: (1) en banc consideration is necessary to secure or maintain uniformity of the court's decisions; or (2) the proceeding involves a question of exceptional importance.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. App. P. 35(a).

**Practical use:** En banc petitions track the two factors verbatim. Circuit splits framed under (1); doctrinal significance under (2).

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

## Drafting Hooks
- Every notice of appeal counts Rule 4 deadlines first; jurisdiction is unforgiving.
- Every appellant's brief tracks Rule 28(a) ten-element skeleton.
- Every emergency stay motion couples Rule 8 with *Nken* four factors.
