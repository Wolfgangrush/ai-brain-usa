## Section-by-Section Digest

### Rule 401 — Test for Relevant Evidence

**Plain-English summary:** Evidence is relevant if (a) it has any tendency to make a fact more or less probable than it would be without the evidence, and (b) the fact is of consequence in determining the action.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> Evidence is relevant if: (a) it has any tendency to make a fact more or less probable than it would be without the evidence; and (b) the fact is of consequence in determining the action.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. Evid. 401.

**Practical use:** Anchor of every relevance objection and every motion *in limine* opposition brief.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 403 — Excluding Relevant Evidence for Prejudice, Confusion, or Other Reasons

**Plain-English summary:** The court may exclude relevant evidence if its probative value is substantially outweighed by a danger of unfair prejudice, confusing the issues, misleading the jury, undue delay, wasting time, or needlessly presenting cumulative evidence.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> The court may exclude relevant evidence if its probative value is substantially outweighed by a danger of one or more of the following: unfair prejudice, confusing the issues, misleading the jury, undue delay, wasting time, or needlessly presenting cumulative evidence.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. Evid. 403.

**Practical use:** Workhorse of motions *in limine*. Note the "substantially outweighed" tilt favours admission.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 702 — Testimony by Expert Witnesses (Daubert)

**Plain-English summary:** A witness qualified as an expert may testify in the form of an opinion if the proponent demonstrates by a preponderance that: (a) the expert's knowledge will help the trier of fact, (b) testimony is based on sufficient facts, (c) testimony is the product of reliable principles and methods, and (d) the expert's opinion reflects a reliable application of those principles and methods to the facts.

**Statutory text (verbatim, post-2023 amendment):**
> A witness who is qualified as an expert by knowledge, skill, experience, training, or education may testify in the form of an opinion or otherwise if the proponent demonstrates to the court that it is more likely than not that: (a) the expert's scientific, technical, or other specialized knowledge will help the trier of fact to understand the evidence or to determine a fact in issue; (b) the testimony is based on sufficient facts or data; (c) the testimony is the product of reliable principles and methods; and (d) the expert's opinion reflects a reliable application of the principles and methods to the facts of the case.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. Evid. 702.

**Practical use:** The 2023 amendment explicitly placed the burden on the proponent and tightened the gatekeeping inquiry. Read with *Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharm., Inc.*, 509 U.S. 579 (1993); *Kumho Tire Co. v. Carmichael*, 526 U.S. 137 (1999); *Gen. Elec. Co. v. Joiner*, 522 U.S. 136 (1997).

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 801(c) — Hearsay Definition

**Plain-English summary:** Hearsay is an out-of-court statement offered to prove the truth of the matter asserted. If either prong fails, the statement is not hearsay.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> "Hearsay" means a statement that: (1) the declarant does not make while testifying at the current trial or hearing; and (2) a party offers in evidence to prove the truth of the matter asserted in the statement.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. Evid. 801(c).

**Practical use:** Every hearsay objection brief begins with the 801(c) two-prong test, then examines Rule 801(d) exclusions and Rule 803/804/807 exceptions in order.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 803(6) — Records of a Regularly Conducted Activity (Business Records)

**Plain-English summary:** A record of an act or event is admissible if (A) made at or near the time by someone with knowledge, (B) kept in the course of regularly conducted activity, (C) making the record was a regular practice, (D) shown by a qualified witness or certification under Rule 902(11)/(12), and (E) the opponent does not show lack of trustworthiness.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> A record of an act, event, condition, opinion, or diagnosis [is admissible] if: (A) the record was made at or near the time by — or from information transmitted by — someone with knowledge; (B) the record was kept in the course of a regularly conducted activity of a business…; (C) making the record was a regular practice of that activity; (D) all these conditions are shown by the testimony of the custodian or another qualified witness, or by a certification…; and (E) the opponent does not show that the source of information or the method or circumstances of preparation indicate a lack of trustworthiness.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. Evid. 803(6).

**Practical use:** The most-used hearsay exception. Combined with Rule 902(11) self-authenticating certifications for streamlined admission.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 901(a) — Authentication

**Plain-English summary:** To satisfy the requirement of authenticating an item, the proponent must produce evidence sufficient to support a finding that the item is what the proponent claims it is.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> To satisfy the requirement of authenticating or identifying an item of evidence, the proponent must produce evidence sufficient to support a finding that the item is what the proponent claims it is.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. Evid. 901(a).

**Practical use:** Bar to admission of every exhibit. Rule 901(b) provides ten illustrative methods; Rule 902 lists self-authenticating items including ESI under Rule 902(13)–(14).

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.
