# United Nations Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods (CISG)

## Citation Block
- **Treaty:** United Nations Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods, Vienna, 11 April 1980, 1489 U.N.T.S. 3.
- **U.S. Ratification:** Signed 31 August 1981; ratified 11 December 1986; entered into force for the United States 1 January 1988.
- **Official URL:** [https://uncitral.un.org/en/texts/salegoods/conventions/sale_of_goods/cisg](https://uncitral.un.org/en/texts/salegoods/conventions/sale_of_goods/cisg)
- **Bluebook Citation:** *U.N. Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods, Apr. 11, 1980, 1489 U.N.T.S. 3* (CISG art. [N]).

## Authority Tier
Tier 1 — International Sales Backbone (federal treaty, displaces UCC Article 2 where applicable).

## Currency Block
- **Adopted:** Vienna, 11 April 1980.
- **Contracting States:** 97 as of 2026, covering jurisdictions for the majority of world merchandise trade.
- **Doctrinal currents:** U.S. federal courts treat CISG as self-executing federal law; questions of CISG interpretation arise under federal-question jurisdiction (28 U.S.C. § 1331). CISG Advisory Council Opinions (CISG-AC Opinions Nos. 1–24) are persuasive secondary authority.

## Language
The Convention is authoritative in six languages — Arabic, Chinese, English, French, Russian, Spanish.

## Scope of Digest
- **Applicable to:** Contracts for the sale of goods between parties whose places of business are in different Contracting States (or where rules of private international law lead to the law of a Contracting State), unless excluded.
- **Not applicable to (Art. 2):** Goods bought for personal/family/household use; auctions; on execution or otherwise by authority of law; stocks/shares/investment securities/negotiable instruments/money; ships/vessels/hovercraft/aircraft; electricity.

## Article-by-Article Digest

### Article 1 — Sphere of Application

**Plain-English summary:** The Convention applies to contracts of sale of goods between parties whose places of business are in different States: (a) when the States are Contracting States; or (b) when the rules of private international law lead to the application of the law of a Contracting State.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> (1) This Convention applies to contracts of sale of goods between parties whose places of business are in different States: (a) when the States are Contracting States; or (b) when the rules of private international law lead to the application of the law of a Contracting State.

**Citation format:** CISG art. 1(1).

**Practical use:** The United States made an Article 95 declaration: U.S. is not bound by Article 1(1)(b). So only Article 1(1)(a) applies in U.S. courts — both parties' places of business must be in Contracting States.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Article 6 — Party Autonomy

**Plain-English summary:** The parties may exclude the application of the Convention or, subject to Article 12, derogate from or vary the effect of any of its provisions.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> The parties may exclude the application of this Convention or, subject to article 12, derogate from or vary the effect of any of its provisions.

**Citation format:** CISG art. 6.

**Practical use:** International commercial contracts that intend U.S. UCC or other domestic law to govern MUST contain an explicit CISG-exclusion clause (e.g., "This Agreement shall be governed by the laws of the State of New York, excluding the United Nations Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods").

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Article 25 — Fundamental Breach

**Plain-English summary:** A breach of contract committed by one of the parties is fundamental if it results in such detriment to the other party as substantially to deprive him of what he is entitled to expect under the contract, unless the party in breach did not foresee and a reasonable person of the same kind in the same circumstances would not have foreseen such a result.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> A breach of contract committed by one of the parties is fundamental if it results in such detriment to the other party as substantially to deprive him of what he is entitled to expect under the contract, unless the party in breach did not foresee and a reasonable person of the same kind in the same circumstances would not have foreseen such a result.

**Citation format:** CISG art. 25.

**Practical use:** Gateway to avoidance under Arts. 49 (buyer) and 64 (seller); analogous to UCC § 2-612 "substantial impairment" but framed differently.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Article 35 — Conformity of the Goods

**Plain-English summary:** The seller must deliver goods that are of the quantity, quality, and description required by the contract and that are contained or packaged in the manner required. Unless otherwise agreed, goods do not conform unless they: (a) are fit for the purposes for which goods of the same description would ordinarily be used; (b) are fit for any particular purpose made known to the seller at the time of contracting; (c) possess the qualities of goods held out to the buyer as a sample or model; (d) are contained or packaged in the manner usual for such goods.

**Citation format:** CISG art. 35.

**Practical use:** Analog to UCC § 2-314 (implied merchantability) and § 2-315 (fitness for particular purpose). Buyer must examine goods (Art. 38) and give notice of lack of conformity (Art. 39) — failure to do so within a reasonable time bars the buyer's remedies.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Article 79 — Exemption from Damages (Impediment Beyond Control)

**Plain-English summary:** A party is not liable for a failure to perform any of his obligations if he proves that the failure was due to an impediment beyond his control and that he could not reasonably be expected to have taken the impediment into account at the time of the conclusion of the contract or to have avoided or overcome it or its consequences.

**Statutory text (verbatim, partial):**
> (1) A party is not liable for a failure to perform any of his obligations if he proves that the failure was due to an impediment beyond his control and that he could not reasonably be expected to have taken the impediment into account at the time of the conclusion of the contract or to have avoided or overcome it or its consequences.

**Citation format:** CISG art. 79(1).

**Practical use:** International force-majeure analog. Notice obligation under Art. 79(4) within a reasonable time.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

## Drafting Hooks
- Every international sale-of-goods agreement contains an explicit CISG election or exclusion under Art. 6.
- Every nonconformity claim runs through Art. 35 with timely Art. 38/39 notice.
- Every international force-majeure notice tracks Art. 79.
