## Section-by-Section Digest

### Rule 1 — Scope and Purpose

**Plain-English summary:** The rules govern all civil actions in U.S. District Courts and must be construed to secure the just, speedy, and inexpensive determination of every action.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> These rules govern the procedure in all civil actions and proceedings in the United States district courts, except as stated in Rule 81. They should be construed, administered, and employed by the court and the parties to secure the just, speedy, and inexpensive determination of every action and proceeding.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. Civ. P. 1.

**Practical use:** Quoted in proportionality arguments under Rule 26(b)(1) and in opposing dilatory motion practice.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 8(a) — Claims for Relief

**Plain-English summary:** A pleading that states a claim must contain (1) a short and plain statement of jurisdiction, (2) a short and plain statement showing entitlement to relief, and (3) a demand for relief.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> A pleading that states a claim for relief must contain: (1) a short and plain statement of the grounds for the court's jurisdiction…; (2) a short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief; and (3) a demand for the relief sought…

**Citation format:** Fed. R. Civ. P. 8(a).

**Practical use:** Read with *Bell Atl. Corp. v. Twombly*, 550 U.S. 544 (2007), and *Ashcroft v. Iqbal*, 556 U.S. 662 (2009). Every complaint must allege facts plausibly entitling the plaintiff to relief — labels and conclusions are insufficient.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 12(b) — Defenses; Pre-Answer Motions

**Plain-English summary:** Lists seven defenses that may be raised by motion before answering — most importantly (1) lack of subject-matter jurisdiction, (2) lack of personal jurisdiction, (3) improper venue, (5) insufficient service, and (6) failure to state a claim.

**Statutory text (verbatim, partial):**
> Every defense to a claim for relief in any pleading must be asserted in the responsive pleading… But a party may assert the following defenses by motion: (1) lack of subject-matter jurisdiction; (2) lack of personal jurisdiction; (3) improper venue; (4) insufficient process; (5) insufficient service of process; (6) failure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted; (7) failure to join a party under Rule 19.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. Civ. P. 12(b)(1)–(7).

**Practical use:** Rule 12(b)(6) is the workhorse motion to dismiss. Rule 12(g)(2) requires consolidation of available 12(b) defenses or they are waived under Rule 12(h)(1).

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 26(b)(1) — Scope of Discovery; Proportionality

**Plain-English summary:** Parties may obtain discovery of any nonprivileged matter relevant to a claim or defense and proportional to the needs of the case, considering importance of the issues, amount in controversy, parties' access to information, parties' resources, importance of discovery in resolving issues, and whether burden outweighs likely benefit.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> Parties may obtain discovery regarding any nonprivileged matter that is relevant to any party's claim or defense and proportional to the needs of the case, considering the importance of the issues at stake in the action, the amount in controversy, the parties' relative access to relevant information, the parties' resources, the importance of the discovery in resolving the issues, and whether the burden or expense of the proposed discovery outweighs its likely benefit.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. Civ. P. 26(b)(1).

**Practical use:** Anchor of every proportionality objection. The 2015 amendment moved "proportional to the needs of the case" from Rule 26(b)(2)(C) into the scope clause itself.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 56(a) — Summary Judgment Standard

**Plain-English summary:** The court must grant summary judgment if the movant shows there is no genuine dispute as to any material fact and the movant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> A party may move for summary judgment, identifying each claim or defense — or the part of each claim or defense — on which summary judgment is sought. The court shall grant summary judgment if the movant shows that there is no genuine dispute as to any material fact and the movant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. Civ. P. 56(a).

**Practical use:** Read with *Celotex Corp. v. Catrett*, 477 U.S. 317 (1986), and *Anderson v. Liberty Lobby*, 477 U.S. 242 (1986). Every motion brief must identify materials under Rule 56(c)(1) and address admissibility under Rule 56(c)(2).

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 65 — Injunctions and Restraining Orders

**Plain-English summary:** Governs temporary restraining orders (TROs) and preliminary injunctions. TROs may issue ex parte only on a showing of immediate and irreparable injury and only for 14 days (extendable once for good cause); preliminary injunctions require notice and an opportunity to be heard.

**Statutory text (verbatim, partial):**
> The court may issue a temporary restraining order without written or oral notice to the adverse party… only if: (A) specific facts in an affidavit or a verified complaint clearly show that immediate and irreparable injury, loss, or damage will result… before the adverse party can be heard in opposition; and (B) the movant's attorney certifies in writing any efforts made to give notice and the reasons why it should not be required.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. Civ. P. 65(b)(1).

**Practical use:** Combined with the *Winter v. NRDC*, 555 U.S. 7 (2008), four-factor test (likelihood of success · irreparable harm · balance of equities · public interest) in every injunction brief.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

## Drafting Hooks
- Every complaint cites Rule 8(a); every 12(b)(6) brief tracks *Twombly/Iqbal* plausibility language.
- Every discovery objection invokes Rule 26(b)(1) proportionality factors.
- Every TRO / PI brief recites the *Winter* four factors under Rule 65.
