## Section-by-Section Digest

### Rule 11(b) — Considering and Accepting a Guilty Plea

**Plain-English summary:** Before accepting a guilty plea, the court must address the defendant personally, ensure the plea is voluntary, and confirm there is a factual basis.

**Statutory text (verbatim, partial):**
> Before the court accepts a plea of guilty or nolo contendere, the defendant may be placed under oath, and the court must address the defendant personally in open court… [and] must determine that the plea is voluntary and did not result from force, threats, or promises (other than promises in a plea agreement)… Before entering judgment on a guilty plea, the court must determine that there is a factual basis for the plea.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. Crim. P. 11(b).

**Practical use:** Cornerstone of plea-withdrawal motions under Rule 11(d) and post-conviction challenges under 28 U.S.C. § 2255.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 12(b)(3) — Pretrial Motions That Must Be Raised Before Trial

**Plain-English summary:** Specified defenses (defects in instituting prosecution, defects in the indictment, motion to suppress, severance, discovery) must be raised before trial or are waived absent good cause.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> The following defenses, objections, and requests must be raised by pretrial motion if the basis for the motion is then reasonably available and the motion can be determined without a trial on the merits: (A) a defect in instituting the prosecution…; (B) a defect in the indictment or information…; (C) suppression of evidence; (D) severance of charges or defendants under Rule 14; and (E) discovery under Rule 16.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. Crim. P. 12(b)(3).

**Practical use:** Every motion to suppress under the Fourth Amendment runs through Rule 12(b)(3)(C).

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 16(a)(1) — Government's Disclosure (Discovery)

**Plain-English summary:** On request, the government must permit inspection of the defendant's statements, prior record, documents, expert disclosures, and items material to preparing the defense.

**Statutory text (verbatim, partial):**
> Upon a defendant's request, the government must disclose to the defendant the substance of any relevant oral statement made by the defendant… [and] disclose to the defendant a copy of the defendant's prior criminal record… [and] permit the defendant to inspect and to copy or photograph books, papers, documents, data, photographs, tangible objects, buildings or places, or copies or portions of any of these items, if the item is within the government's possession, custody, or control and: (i) the item is material to preparing the defense; (ii) the government intends to use the item in its case-in-chief…; or (iii) the item was obtained from or belongs to the defendant.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. Crim. P. 16(a)(1).

**Practical use:** Read alongside *Brady v. Maryland*, 373 U.S. 83 (1963), and *Giglio v. United States*, 405 U.S. 150 (1972), for the constitutional disclosure baseline.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 29(a) — Judgment of Acquittal

**Plain-English summary:** After the government closes its evidence or after the close of all evidence, the court must enter judgment of acquittal on any offense for which the evidence is insufficient to sustain a conviction.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> After the government closes its evidence or after the close of all the evidence, the court on the defendant's motion must enter a judgment of acquittal of any offense for which the evidence is insufficient to sustain a conviction. The court may on its own consider whether the evidence is insufficient to sustain a conviction.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. Crim. P. 29(a).

**Practical use:** Preserves sufficiency challenge on appeal. Standard: viewing evidence in light most favorable to government, no rational trier of fact could find each element beyond a reasonable doubt — *Jackson v. Virginia*, 443 U.S. 307 (1979).

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 32 — Sentencing and Judgment

**Plain-English summary:** Governs presentence investigation (Rule 32(c)–(g)), sentencing hearing procedure (Rule 32(i)), right of allocution (Rule 32(i)(4)), and judgment (Rule 32(k)).

**Statutory text (verbatim, partial):**
> Before imposing sentence, the court must: (A) verify that the defendant and the defendant's attorney have read and discussed the presentence report…; (B) allow the defendant's attorney to speak on the defendant's behalf; (C) address the defendant personally in order to permit the defendant to speak or present any information to mitigate the sentence…

**Citation format:** Fed. R. Crim. P. 32(i)(4).

**Practical use:** Combined with U.S. Sentencing Guidelines and 18 U.S.C. § 3553(a) sentencing factors in every sentencing memorandum.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.

---

### Rule 41(g) — Motion to Return Property

**Plain-English summary:** A person aggrieved by an unlawful search and seizure or by the deprivation of property may move for the property's return.

**Statutory text (verbatim):**
> A person aggrieved by an unlawful search and seizure of property or by the deprivation of property may move for the property's return. The motion must be filed in the district where the property was seized.

**Citation format:** Fed. R. Crim. P. 41(g).

**Practical use:** Vehicle for civil-forfeiture challenges and equitable post-seizure return.

**STATUS:** VERIFIED.
